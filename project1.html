    • E-Hop

    Project Description

    In the Fall of 2023, I took IAT267 and was assigned a final project in groups of 4 to create a smart home device, health monitoring device or an educational toy for children. Multiple sensor usage was mandatory, powered by Arduino Microcontroller and Processing to display the user interface. My group and I decided to make an educational toy for children called E-Hop, a digital iteration of the traditional hopscotch game.

    We as a group decided to go with E-Hop because we found that the first five years of a child are the most crucial stage for physical development. Moreover, five to six percent of children, which amounts to two children in every classroom in Canada, are affected by Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD) or Dyspraxia. It is a condition where children are severely uncoordinated for their age. Through E-Hop, we aim to provide early intervention to minimize the risk of getting DCD for children prone to the disease in a fun and educative method.

    Ideation Process

    I am responsible for choosing which sensors to use to build the device. Initially, I planned on using a force sensor to register input given by the user, however, after a discussion with my instructor, he recommended using Velostat, a pressure-sensitive conductive sheet.

    Prototype

    I am responsible for choosing which sensors to use to build the device. Initially, I planned on using a force sensor to register input given by the user, however, after a discussion with my instructor, he recommended using Velostat, a pressure-sensitive conductive sheet.

    Initial Assembly

    Now that the prototype is fully working, I, with my groupmate's help, start assembling the sensors and wire them to the Arduino board. I ensured the overall build quality was sturdy enough not to break when the user stepped on it.

    Code

    The next step is while my groupmates design the user interface, I am responsible for code development in Processing. This involves creating levels, reading sensor inputs, and generating interactions based on user feedback, such as buzzer sounds and level progressions.

    Final Installation

    The project is a success, and many students, including instructors, visit my booth to give it a shot. Many improvements could be made in the input and sensors, but I am delighted with the end product. Interactivity with the users matters most, and I successfully achieved it through my work.

    Final Reflection

    With all the time given and the research my group and I did, this project was a success, and I am very proud of it. I can add more output to improve the installation, such as lights or buzzers, to show users whether the mat recognizes their feedback. Still, with the time restrictions and unfamiliarity of the velostat, I am pleased with me and my group's work.

    Our approach to making games is to find the fun first and then use the technology to enhance the fun.
